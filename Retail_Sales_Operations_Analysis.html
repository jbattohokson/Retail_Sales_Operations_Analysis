Executive Summary

Between December 2014 and July 2016, this retail portfolio experienced a 10x revenue surge in a single month (August 2015). This analysis confirms the jump was structural, not a data artifact. Monthly revenue shifted from a sub-$50K baseline to a sustained $460–500K average and stayed there.

The surge, however, exposed a profitability problem hiding underneath the revenue growth. Bikes, the highest-volume category in the largest market (US), operate at a negative gross margin of -1.5%. Every dollar of US Bike revenue currently destroys value at the gross profit line. At $650K in Road Bike revenue alone, that represents approximately $9,750 in gross losses before operating expenses (calculated as $650K x -1.5%; assumes current volume holds).

The path to margin recovery does not require cutting Bike revenue. It requires repricing US Bike SKUs and cross-selling Accessories (which carry up to 22% gross margin) to the demographic segment already spending the most: female customers aged 45–54.

Analysis

What the data shows, and what it means

01 August 2015 was a structural inflection point, not a seasonal spike

Monthly revenue held below $50K from December 2014 through July 2015, with minimal variance. In August 2015, revenue jumped to approximately $435K in a single month, a 10x increase. The 3-month rolling average confirms the shift held: post-August revenue averaged $460–500K per month through the rest of the observation window.

The final data point (July 2016: ~$210K) is almost certainly a data truncation artifact, not a real revenue contraction. Both the revenue signal and the margin signal drop simultaneously in that month, which is consistent with a partial-month data pull at period-end rather than a business event.

Interpretation

Something changed in August 2015: a new product line, a distribution channel opening, a pricing change, or a market expansion. The data confirms the inflection was real and sustained. Finance should identify the business event that caused it and use it as a baseline for forecasting future expansion scenarios. A Chow test would formally verify the structural break and support that business case.

02 US Bike sales are loss-generating at the gross profit line

The United States is almost certainly the largest revenue market by volume. Its core high-volume category (Bikes) operates at a −1.5% gross margin. Road Bikes alone generated approximately $650K in revenue. At −1.5%, that translates to roughly $9,750 in gross losses on $650K of revenue, before any operating costs are applied.

What this means for revenue planning

A US Bike pricing audit is the highest-ROI action available from this dataset. The question for the pricing team is specific: what price increase on US Bike SKUs is needed to reach breakeven at current volume, and at what point does volume elasticity make that increase self-defeating? Germany's 16.7% Bike margin on the same product category suggests the margin gap is a pricing and cost structure problem, not an inherent product-category limitation.

03 Accessories are the margin engine, but they're being undersold to the highest-value customers

Accessories dominate portfolio profitability post-August 2015, accounting for the majority of stacked gross margin in every month through mid-2016. Fenders and Bike Racks carry the highest margins in the entire product portfolio (21–22%) but generate minimal revenue in absolute terms, sitting in the low-revenue / high-margin quadrant.

What this means for targeting

Female customers aged 45–54 are the highest-LTV segment in the portfolio and they are currently shopping a low-margin category (Bikes) more than a high-margin one (Accessories). A targeted campaign bundling Helmets, Fenders, and Bottles & Cages with Bike purchases, aimed specifically at this cohort, improves blended margin without requiring a price increase or volume growth. If 10% of US Bike transactions added a $50 Accessories attach, that's approximately $3,250 in incremental high-margin revenue on the current volume base.

04 Germany outperforms the US on margin by 15+ percentage points across every category

Germany achieves 30.3% on Accessories and 28.6% on Clothing, roughly double the US margins in both categories. Bike margin in Germany is 16.7% versus −1.5% in the US. This is an 18.2 percentage point gap on identical product categories sold across different markets.

What this means for market expansion

The Germany margin advantage is either a pricing difference, a cost structure difference, or a product mix difference, and that distinction matters for strategy. If Germany charges more for the same SKUs, that's a US underpricing problem. If Germany's cost structure is lower (freight, distribution, duties), that's an operational opportunity. If Germany sells more high-margin Accessories per transaction, that's a bundling and merchandising opportunity. A follow-on analysis pulling SKU-level unit price by market would separate these three explanations and quantify the expansion opportunity.

05 The 25–34 cohort drives volume; the 45–54 cohort drives margin opportunity

The 25–34 age band is the dominant revenue segment at approximately $1.95M, nearly 20% above the next-highest group (35–44 at ~$1.6M). Revenue declines sharply at 55–64 (~$360K) and is negligible at 65+.

What this means for campaign strategy

Don't confuse volume with value. The 25–34 cohort is the biggest spender in aggregate, but it's also the most competitive segment to acquire. The 45–54 cohort is female-dominant, already converting, and buying in a category where this portfolio has a margin advantage if cross-sells are executed. Marketing dollars allocated to the 45–54 female segment, specifically toward Accessories, generate higher margin per acquisition dollar than the same spend on the 25–34 volume segment.

Statistical Models

Two OLS models: what each one measures

M1 Time Series Model: Does revenue trend upward over time?

A linear OLS regression was fitted on the monthly revenue time series, using a sequential time index as the sole predictor.

R² = 0.674 Slope = +$29,500 / month p-value < 0.001 n = 20 months

Model limitation -- read before using this number

The R² of 0.674 overstates model quality. The residual plot shows a systematic pattern: negative in early months, consistently positive post-August 2015, sharply negative at period-end. This is a signature of temporal autocorrelation and structural non-stationarity. The linear model treats a two-regime time series as one continuous trend. The $29,500/month slope is accurate as a whole-period average, but it should not be used for point forecasting without fitting a piecewise model at the August 2015 breakpoint.

M2 Demographic Model: What drives revenue at the transaction level?

A cross-sectional OLS regression was built at the demographic segment level, using age band, gender, product category, and quantity as predictors of revenue per transaction.

What the model found

Gender is the single largest positive revenue coefficient in the demographic model. Female customers, controlling for age and product category, are associated with higher per-transaction revenue. This result drives the Accessories cross-sell recommendation toward the 45–54 female cohort; it's not just a demographic observation; it's a model-supported targeting signal.

Technical Reference

Pipeline architecture & data scope

This analysis was produced by a three-step pipeline. Each stage is independently auditable. Source code is available on GitHub.

01_acs_etl.py

Reshapes raw wide-format Excel (weekly columns × measure rows) into a flat analytical table. Derives gross profit, margin, date dimensions, and age bands. Flags geographic data quality issues before output.

02_acs_setup.sql

Loads cleaned CSV into a MySQL schema. Applies four exclusion filters: null states, cities filed as states, non-approved sub-categories, and zero-revenue rows. Builds production views and Tableau-ready aggregates.

03_acs_analysis.py

Queries DB views, exports Tableau CSVs, runs both OLS regression models, and produces the residuals panel. Inline interpretation of p-values and R² makes output self-contained for non-technical reviewers.
